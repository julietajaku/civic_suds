Laundromat Literacy Feature on PBS

April 2, 2019

PBS News Hour ran this story tonight about laundromat literacy, featuring video footage from some of my past efforts in extending literacy programs to coin laundry waiting areas in New York. Here’s what I shared in this feature:

“We are looking for behavioral shifts, because those are long-lasting, and those will follow these parents and these kids way beyond the laundromat.”

Yes! Long-lasting change is what excites me about meeting families where they are in consistent and thoughtful ways, and this continues to be my goal with Civic Suds.

My favorite line in this feature is from laundromat client and grandmother Lillian Pierce Hunt: “It starts with stuff like this, where parents spend time, and they're busy, and it's not that you don't want to read to your kids. You just don't have the time.”

Allister Chang - Founder, Civic Suds

Why Laundromats?

March 28, 2019

This past year, I have spent most weekends sitting inside laundromats. My friends and family have generally responded with confusion. They do everything they can to avoid laundromats, but I can’t help myself from wandering inside laundromats whenever I pass one. I have fallen in love with laundromats and the potential that they offer for the future of non-formal education and community building.

Between 2014-2018, I worked as the Executive Director of Libraries Without Borders. In this capacity, I had opportunities to partner with public libraries across the US to text new programs. My dream job after completing my studies in public policy at Harvard. I woke up every morning thinking about how to re-imagine libraries for the 21st century, and then actually test those ideas. One of my crazy ideas — expanding public library programs to coin laundromat waiting areas — ended up working! Actually, like most of my ideas, librarians had already tested this idea. My contribution was to formalize, standardize, evaluate, improve and share best-practices with public librarians across the US (learn more about the Wash & Learn Initiative here).

Laundromats, we found, were the best place to offer a range of educational programs for families. When you go to the laundromat, you have to wait 90+ minutes. This offers laundromat clients a convenient time to apply for jobs, learn how to read, do taxes, sign up for health insurance, etc. etc. etc. The possibilities are endless!

BUT, most laundromats across the US are purely profit-driven, trying to squeeze every penny out of their clients. I am building Civic Suds in order to flip that model — to put civic responsibilities at the forefront of the business model. Civic Suds will channel 100% of its profits to support educational opportunities for the local community. In doing so, I have two main goals:

  • Revolutionize the laundromat industry, which hasn’t really changed since the 1950s

  • Create financially self-sustaining & recession-resistant community centers

I also have a bunch of other goals, including, but not at all limited to:

  • Providing accessible opportunities for community members to improve their literacy, digital literacy, media literacy, financial literacy, and health literacyRevolutionize the laundromat industry, which hasn’t really changed since the 1950s

  • Providing public access to Internet connectivity and computers

  • Rebuilding trust between people across diverse communities, as well as between people and local institutions

  • Offering local non-profits, families, entrepreneurs — everyone! — free workspace and meeting rooms

If you’re asking how/why I think I could possibly be qualified to accomplish all these lofty goals. You’re right! I’m not. At least not alone. I can’t do it without the support of many many more smart people, and I hope you’ll be one of those people that wants to help build a branch of Civic Suds in your local community.

Allister Chang - Founder, Civic Suds
